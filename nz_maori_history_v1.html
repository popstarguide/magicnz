The Māori are recognised as the first humans to migrate to New Zealand. Their story is one of extraordinary navigation, a rich and complex culture, and a resilience that continues to shape Aotearoa today.

Arriving in Aotearoa

The Great Migration

According to Māori legend — following in the footsteps of their great ancestor Kupe — the Māori left Hawaiiki in 1340 AD, sailing in 7 waka (canoes) to Aotearoa. Each waka landed in a different part of the country, and their descendants became the different Iwi (tribes) found around New Zealand today.

The 7 Waka

Horouta · Takitimu · Mataatua · Mamari

Arawa · Tainui · Aotea

Historians believe that in reality there were many voyages over a period stretching from about 940 AD to 1400 AD — a sustained and deliberate settling of a new land.

~1500 AD

Classical Māori Culture

The Māori initially lived a nomadic lifestyle, following food sources and seasons. Around 1500 AD, as natural food sources began to disappear, they shifted to more land-based foods — including the Kūmara (sweet potato) and pigs they had brought with them from Hawaiiki.

This was the era in which classical Māori culture emerged: the Wharetupuna (ornately carved ancestral meeting house), the Pā (fortified village), sophisticated warfare, and a spiritual world rich with multiple gods, Tohunga (spiritual experts akin to shamans), and a deep empathy for the natural environment — parallels that have often been drawn with the beliefs of North American First Nations peoples.

Two words that define a culture

Mana & Utu

▸ Mana — self-worth and prestige, but not self-declared. Mana is how others perceive you based on how you conduct yourself and the respect you show others. You can act in ways that affect your mana, but ultimately it is bestowed upon you by your community.

▸ Utu — reciprocal action, always one step better than what was received. Not purely revenge — it works positively too. If you visit someone's whare (house) and receive a cup of tea, when they visit you, you must offer tea and cake. Next visit: tea, cake and dinner. Fail to reciprocate well, and your mana suffers.

It's easy to see how small matters of utu could escalate into full intertribal battles — but it also made generosity contagious.

Warriors of the stone age

Ahead of Their Time

The first Europeans to encounter the Māori described them as the finest hand-to-hand combat fighters they had ever seen. By the 1860s, the Māori were employing camouflage, guerrilla warfare and trench warfare — while the British were still wearing red uniforms and marching in open lines to shoot at each other.

The Taiaha

A weapon that resembles a spear — but a warrior would never throw it. It was his most prized possession.

Used to strike the weak points of an opponent — knees, elbows — until they could no longer fight. The finishing blow came from a patu or mere (handheld club).

Brutal — yes. But remember this was a stone-age culture. Europeans didn't bring the first metal tools and weapons until 1770 AD.

The Māori are an incredibly proud and defiant people — who also know how to have fun, with stories and waiata (song). You will discover this as you explore New Zealand.

· · ·

European colonisation

An Up and Down Time of Things

Like many colonised peoples, the Māori had their land taken by whatever means were available. Many signed the Treaty of Waitangi in good faith, believing their interests would be protected — only to be badly mistaken. This led to the New Zealand Wars of the late 1800s, as Māori took up arms to defend their land while their politicians pleaded with a government that didn't afford them sufficient mana to be heard.

By the late 1800s, disease introduced through colonisation — influenza, childhood infections, TB, STDs — had devastated the population. The common European view at the time was that the Māori would be extinct by 1930.

The Māori volunteered in significant numbers for World War 2, believing it would earn back some of their land. It did not. Through the 1960s and into the 1980s, they mounted a series of protests over land claims and treaty settlements — this time with Māori lawyers who were both articulate and formidable in the legal arena.

· · ·

The present day

Righting the Wrongs

A Culture Thriving Again

Land has been returned or compensation paid. Tribal settlements have generally been invested back into scholarships so young Māori can access education.

Te Reo Māori is now an official language of New Zealand — and Te Rauparaha's haka is performed by our national sports teams before every important game.

Over nearly 200 years of European colonisation, the Māori have adopted western ways of living and dress. Unlike many countries with indigenous peoples who visually stand out, here you will interact with far more Māori than you may realise — and yet beneath the surface, they hold dear their past and their deep connection to their cultural ways.

The Māori story is one of survival against the odds — and of a culture that refused to disappear.

New Zealand has been compared to an ark adrift on the Pacific Ocean — unaffected by the outside world until about 1000 AD and the arrival of the first humans. The result is a wildlife story found nowhere else on earth.

The birds

A Land of Flightless Birds

The large number of flightless — or not very good at flight — birds here tells you everything: there were no native land-based predators. Being surrounded by ocean definitely stopped land animals from arriving, though water and air-based fauna could come and go with the currents of wind or sea.

The most famous is the Kiwi — a small, defenceless, completely nocturnal flightless bird and the national emblem of New Zealand. Today it is an endangered species. Although kiwi live all over the country, the likelihood of seeing one in the wild is slim — Stewart Island is probably the best chance.

Where to See a Kiwi

Kiwi houses — purpose-built nocturnal viewing buildings — can be found at:

Otorohanga · Mt Bruce · Wellington · Queenstown

Stewart Island remains the best place to encounter a kiwi in the wild.

The mammals

Just Two — Both Bats

Prior to the arrival of humans there were only 2 mammals in New Zealand — two species of fruit bat. Like many of our native animals, they are now endangered. The only real wild population of fruit bats remains in the Fiordland National Park.

The reptiles

The Oldest Living Animal on the Planet

New Zealand has about 30 species of reptile — one of which is the Tuatara, the oldest living animal on the planet. Forget the crocodile — this creature has outlived them all. The species is over 60 million years old.

Today tuatara can only be found in the wild on offshore predator-proof islands — though some may still exist in the remote fiords of the south west of the South Island.

Where to See a Tuatara

Mt Bruce Wildlife Centre — north of Masterton in the Wairarapa

Zealandia — Wellington. You might be lucky and spot one basking in the sun.

The bush

An Evergreen Rainforest

New Zealand benefited from ash-laden fertile soil, no shortage of water, and ideal growing temperatures — around 18–22°C average for most of the country. About 80% of the country was covered in bush at the time of the first humans' arrival. Today around 38% remains forested, with about 79% of that under government protection in National Parks.

The New Zealand bush is an evergreen rainforest — a mixture of flaxes and ferns through to majestic kauri trees and southern beech.

Tane Mahuta — Lord of the Forest

The kauri is a massive tree — especially per volume. One tree in particular has been named Tane Mahuta, meaning Lord of the Forest.

Found in the Waipoua Forest in Northland, Tane Mahuta is the largest tree per volume in the Southern Hemisphere.

In this section I'd like to enlighten you with some of our little delights and nationwide stories of our environment. To truly appreciate the positives — no matter how small — first you need to understand the mistakes of our past.

Before humans arrived

A Paradise Untouched

At the time of Māori arrival New Zealand was about 85% bush — the rest alpine or coast — containing an amazing array of previously unseen species.

Extinct Species

The Moa — a massive flightless bird growing to about 10 feet in height, like an emu or ostrich. The largest land-based animal here, filling the herbivore gap that cows and horses fill elsewhere.

The Haast Eagle — the largest bird of prey that has ever lived. Wingspan of 3.5m, body weight of 15kg, talons 1.5cm long. Its prey? The moa.

There were only 2 species of mammal at the time of Māori arrival — both species of fruit bat.

· · ·

Māori settlement ~1300 AD

The First Changes

The Māori discovered the moa was an easy food source — having had no prior contact with humans, the birds had no instinct of the danger, and their size made them cumbersome in the bush when hunted.

It took the Māori about 150 years to completely hunt all of the moa — in doing so, they removed the food source of the Haast eagle, which died out as well.

The Māori also introduced the rat, pig and dog. The rat wasn't introduced by mistake — it was brought for food during the migration voyage. Being small and easy to feed, it made a good source of meat during months at sea.

However, the impact the Māori made was small compared to what came next. About the time of European arrival there was still about 80% native bush covering the country — much of the burnt bush had regrown.

· · ·

European settlement ~1769 onwards

The Introduced Disasters

The Europeans destroyed massive areas of bush for farmland — and introduced some of the worst environmental disasters this country has ever seen.

▸ Possum — introduced to start a fur trade. Now 70 million nationwide.

▸ Rabbit — also for fur. Bred completely out of control in hard-soiled areas.

▸ Deer — introduced in the early 1800s so the affluent had something to hunt on weekends.

▸ Stoats & Ferrets — introduced to control the rabbit problem. Preferred native birds instead.

▸ Cats — brought as pets, became feral and now prey on native birds.

The Possum Problem

70 million possums nationwide.

They chew through 21,000 tonnes of vegetation per night, eat native chicks and eggs, and carry TB.

In the same timeframe the kiwi population has fallen from 64 million to just 35,000 across 5 species.

What's particularly frustrating — the possum, rabbit, ferret and stoat were all commercially hunted until the early 1980s, keeping populations in check. When the anti-fur movement (for legitimate reasons) killed the fur industry here too, the possum population grew from an estimated 50 million to its current level.

The present day

Reasons for Hope

Time for Some Positives

The Calici virus was introduced into the rabbit population and had a devastating impact — green pastures are now seen in places that were once moving hills of rabbits.

Deer are now largely kept under control by the Department of Conservation through yearly culls and recreational hunters.

Ferrets, stoats and possums are being hunted through traps and bait stations across the country.

⚠️ Heads up for trampers: If you see white paste on posts or stumps while in the bush — don't touch it. It is cyanide bait used in pest control operations.

The 1080 Program — The Results

The aerial 1080 poison drops are controversial but the results speak for themselves:

Native bird chick survival in control areas (no 1080): ~5%

Native bird chick survival in 1080 treated areas: ~95%

Birds are visibly reclaiming areas they once lived in, predator free.

It's a long way to go to return to the utopia this country once was — but with the right processes, we may get some of it back.
